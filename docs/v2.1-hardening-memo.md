# Evaluation of the two research reviews, and what I changed

**Date:** 2026-06-19 · **Scope:** critique of `BISt Info.md` + `Research Review.md` against `system-design-v2.md` and `data-sourcing-v2.md`, and the resulting `system-design-v2.md` → v2.1 patch.

---

## Bottom line

Both reviews are competent literature scans and weak critiques. You commissioned them to stress-test the design; they mostly validate and paraphrase it. By my count roughly **20% of their content is genuinely additive**; the rest is the design read back to you, fashionable graph-ML that does not fit a 500-name problem, or generic governance/market-structure padding. Your instinct in the prompt — that they may be "outdated or poorly evaluated" — is correct on both counts, and I verified the specific places where it bites.

This is not a reason to dismiss them. The 20% is real and worth taking, and one of the catches (IAS 29) is the single most concrete improvement either report surfaced. But you should not read the warm "your design is directionally right, just harden it" framing as independent validation. Two reports reaching the same congratulatory conclusion, both anchored to your own document, is correlated evidence, not corroboration.

## Where they are outdated or wrong (verified)

I web-verified every time-sensitive claim.

- **IAS 29 suspension — the *Research Review* is right, and it is now law.** Turkey's parliament passed a law (Dec 2025) suspending inflation accounting for **FY2025–2027** (annual and interim), with the president able to extend another three years; banks/leasing/factoring/financing were already carved out by the BDDK. This **invalidates a factual assumption in your own §3** ("From FY2023 Turkish issuers report inflation-adjusted financials," treated as enduring). Comparability now breaks at **two** boundaries, not one: nominal → IAS 29 (FY2023) and IAS 29 → suspended (FY2025). This is the best single thing either report produced, and I have patched §3 for it.
- **Short-sale ban "lapsed September 2025" — both reports, wrong month; your sourcing doc, incomplete.** The reality: partial lift Jan 2, 2025 (BIST-50) → full **reimposition March 23, 2025** after the İmamoğlu detention → four extensions → final lift **August 29, 2025** (not September). The ban toggled **6+ times in a single year.** Both reviews undersell this, and so does `data-sourcing-v2.md` (which stops at "partial lift Jan-2025"). The reality is *more* supportive of your design than any document conveys: short-availability is not a caveat, it is a fast-moving per-name/per-date state, and the venue-feasible book is mandatory. Patched §3 and §10.5.
- **March 2025 İmamoğlu market facts (BISt Info) — accurate.** Detention 19 March, lira off >10%, Borsa İstanbul circuit-breaker-halted twice after BIST 100 −6.87%. These hold up.

## Where they are poorly evaluated

1. **Graph neural networks are over-indexed in both.** THGNN / MDGNN / DGRCL / TCGPN / temporal-relational ranking are pushed in both reports. For ~500 names with limited clean Turkish history in an explicit p≈n regime — the very regime your design names as its central statistical hazard — GNNs are close to the worst-fit tool: data-hungry, overfit-prone, hard to keep strictly point-in-time, and benchmark-validated on US/China large universes with deep history. The *Research Review* half-admits this (ranks them "Selective / High effort / High risk") yet still spends real estate on them; *BISt Info* is more credulous. Correct weight: a year-2 curiosity to attempt **only after** the regularized baseline is exhausted and **only if** it beats the naive-baseline ladder (which it likely will not at this universe size). Not a near-term upgrade.
2. **Enterprise governance imported wholesale.** The *Research Review* recommends NIST AI RMF (Govern–Map–Measure–Manage), model-governance committees, and role separation. This is MLOps governance for a regulated multi-stakeholder org; your project is a personal, non-commercial research substrate run by one person. The kernel worth keeping — monitor extraction error, drift, and provenance decay — is already implied by your provenance principle (§2.5). The committee scaffolding is theatre here. Federated learning (also raised) is irrelevant to a single-user system; it is correctly self-flagged but should not have been included at all.
3. **Market-structure trivia presented as open risks.** *BISt Info* devotes comparison-table rows to US-vs-BIST **trading hours** and **T+1 settlement**, and lists settlement/tax/foreign-ownership as "open questions to resolve before production." For a **daily**, close-to-close research system these are near-irrelevant — intraday session structure and settlement timing do not touch daily-return alpha research — and several are already resolved in your sourcing pass. This is padding to look comprehensive, and it misdirects attention.
4. **They were blind to your own newer work.** Both recommend "LLM NER / link-prediction to build supply-chain edges." Useful in the abstract, but your 2026-06-19 sourcing pass already found a concrete, structured, materiality-weighted **tier-1** source neither report knew about: KAP "Yeni İş İlişkisi" (new-business) disclosures via Matriks `includeNewBusiness` (counterparty + amount + date + materiality ratios). Their supply-chain advice is partly obsolete relative to what you already have.
5. **The citation apparatus is unverifiable.** Both are littered with `cite…turn…academia…` / `…file0` tokens — leaked internal deep-research markers, not followable references — and lean heavily on arXiv preprints, which the *Research Review* itself concedes are benchmark-centric and not deployment-validated. Treat any paper-backed claim as a lead to check, not a settled finding.

## Risks neither report flagged (the critique they should have written)

A genuinely adversarial review would have pressed on the four places this system is most likely to fail. None of these appear in either document.

1. **The residual may not survive the foreign-flow strip.** Your most dangerous hypothesis is your own: that BIST comovement is dominated by foreign risk-on/off flow. If you correctly neutralize market + FX + foreign-flow, there may be very little stable *residual* linkage left — i.e., the correlation pillar's "alpha" could largely *be* the flow factor you removed. This is testable early and should be a go/no-go gate on the lead pillar, not a footnote.
2. **Transaction cost, borrow, and capacity on thin names.** The signals concentrate where liquidity is thin — exactly where stat-arb and lead-lag look great in backtests and die live. "Net of costs" is hand-waving without an explicit per-name cost model, a borrow/short-eligibility join, and capacity curves. This deserves a number before any pillar is believed.
3. **Linked-firm predictability is US-documented, not BIST-proven.** The Cohen–Frazzini / Menzly–Özbaş effect is your most novel edge and rests on the least-tested premise. In Turkey, intra-group trade is related-party and opaque, and the group structure is *already visible* — the lag may already be priced. Treat the supply-chain pillar as a hypothesis to falsify, not a payoff to harvest.
4. **Event cross-sections may collapse.** When the dominant channel (FX/political) hits everything at once — as in March 2025 — clean "low-exposure control" names barely exist, and the differential-exposure spread thins out precisely in the events that matter most. Quantify how many usable control names survive a typical Turkish event before trusting the design.

A fifth, operational: **the identifier bridge (ticker ↔ ISIN ↔ mkkMemberOid ↔ LEI) is a single point of failure** across L1/L2. A silent mismatch corrupts every cross-store join and every backtest built on it. Your sourcing doc flags it; neither review did. Make it a tested, first-class object with reconciliation invariants.

The edits are surgical hardening, not a redesign — which is the honest conclusion once you discount the parts of the reviews that were echoing you back.
